6 • In-Depth Research: The Antikythera Mechanism – The World’s Oldest Known Analog Computer

The Antikythera Mechanism is a remarkable bronze gearwork device recovered from a shipwreck off the Greek island of Antikythera in 1901. Dated to around 100 BCE, it is the oldest known example of a mechanical analog computer. Its intricate system of at least 30 meshing gears could predict the positions of the Sun, Moon, and planets, calculate eclipses, and even track the four-year cycle of the Olympic Games. Official Overview

The mechanism was found in fragments inside an ancient Roman cargo ship that sank around 70–60 BCE. X-ray and CT scanning in the early 2000s by researchers at the National Archaeological Museum in Athens and University College London revealed its astonishing complexity — far beyond anything previously known from the ancient world. 1

Scholars now believe it was likely designed by a Greek astronomer or mathematician, possibly inspired by the work of Hipparchus or Archimedes. It functioned as a portable planetarium and calendar, demonstrating a level of technological sophistication that was not matched again until the mechanical clocks of medieval Europe. 2

The Antikythera Mechanism stands as the ultimate Wunderkammer artifact — a physical object that proves the ancient Greeks possessed engineering knowledge once thought impossible. It challenges our assumptions about the history of science and technology and reminds us that the past still holds surprises that can rewrite what we think we know about human ingenuity.
